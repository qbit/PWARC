Field Day After Action Report (WA0KAQ)

  • Some of the basic goals from the preceding year have been used for this report. Many of the things that need more attention have been taken from member comments.

    • Things that went well:

      • We had a safe event.

      • The solar power/battery setup worked well.
      • Tear down only took about half an hour.
      • We did not lose any operating time this year due to storms. Last year we lost about two hours due to lightning in the area.
      • Lindon brought a tent that was used for storage.
      • Restroom facilities were available in the fire station.
      • The club thanked David Yeater for his hard work to get publicity for the club and getting officials from agencies to attend. He took over a job that was badly needed.
      • Having an experienced CW contester in our group certainly bolstered our score again this year. Dan made 129 contacts and showed that the oldest mode is often the best when using low power.
      • Although it was a long shot, we were unable to make the satellite contact we had hoped to make. However, the attempt did help to get our story on the front page of the Pueblo Chieftain.

    • Things that need improvement:

      • We were almost a half an hour late starting the event.

      • Our visitor count was down from 22 last year to 10 who signed in this year.
      • The club banner was in a very poor location. It could only be read if the traffic was coming from McCulloch Blvd. Traffic going toward McCulloch Blvd. only saw the back side. It was also too far away from the site. Use the stand that was built for it and place it where it will do the most good.
      • Even though our club grew by approximately 50% since last year, the number of participants grew by only one person.
      • Digital contacts decreased dramatically from last year, and this hurt the club's score.

    • Comments from members:

      • A complete station (radio, laptop, etc.) needs to be on stand-by at the site in case of failure of any of the stations. The failure of a laptop (unable to take a charge or run on battery power) and something going wrong with either the radio or keyer brought down our CW station. Fortunately, I had my Icom 706 and laptop in the car and we were able to get the station up and running. Unfortunately, however, I wasn't there when the laptop died and manual logging became necessary for some time, which was an inconvenience.

      • Better spacing of our antennas may have helped reduce some interference between rigs.
      • I think the choice of using a vertical and a dipole was a good idea, however some thought might be given to have a way to switch the antennas between rigs to see what is working best from time to time. This might have been advantageous.
      • A better system of measuring the battery voltage available needs to be found. I monitored the CW station Sunday morning for a little while, and the battery voltage was so low that the signal was terribly raspy and very wide.
      • Laptop screens were basically unreadable during daylight hours. Some way of darkening the areas around the computers need to be found.
      • There was no way to keep three stations on the air all night, as there weren't enough operators who stayed over. If you are going to have three stations, you need six operators during the night to trade off sleeping and operating.
      • You may be familiar with your equipment, but when you drop it off with no operating instructions or information on how to hook up cables to various pieces of gear, it doesn't help the effort. At least make a cheat sheet and a diagram of cable hookups to send with it and MARK YOUR CABLES!!
      • Let's try to standardize the PSK31 program, or at least the macros, so when someone sits down to operate your rig he will know how to use it. Everyone bringing a rig for PSK31 needs to add a "chat" macro whereby the operator can ask for information he did not receive or answer other stations' questions.
      • A sheet of WRITTEN information about what is happening at FIELD DAY (not HAMFEST) should be available to give to media personnel. If we want them to be there to tell our story, they need to receive the correct information.
      • Six meters was open, yet no one took advantage of it either with one of the three stations or an additional station that would not have counted against our entry Class.
      • Last year we only operated one station at night because an 80 meter antenna that was promised didn't show up. This year we had an 80 meter antenna available, and not one contact was made on that band.
      • We need more signs, balloons&ldots;. something to attract the public's attention.
      • There should be a co-chairman joined at the hip to the Field Day chairman in case the chairman can't be there, as happened this year.
      • We need to assign times for operators to work the station so that we have help at night.
      • There should be more input to the committee by those who are the best in each mode.
      • Someone should be in charge of each mode and be responsible for having everything ready for that mode to run at Field Day.
      • Having a memory keyer for working CW would make the task easier, as would a rig with a CW filter in it.
      • Those who are using the logging program should have a few days ahead of the event to put it in their computer and verify that the program saves to the computer's drive. One set of scores was almost lost this year in a computer running Vista.
      • We need to somehow inform the public how ham radio can be important to them. Signs at Field Day should encourage their activity in the event and tell them how ham radio affects them.

    • Lessons Learned:

      • When things don't work, "Improvise, Adapt, Overcome." The team did well under the circumstances.

      • A "Go Kit" arrangement for each station used would have provided much more time for operating the equipment. All the jumpers and accessories are already hooked up. All one would have to do is hook up the antenna, plug in one power cable and the labeled cables to the computer and you have everything ready to go on any mode you can operate.

      • Headphones are essential.
