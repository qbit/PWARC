News from the members: (What have you done in the last month that pertains to amateur radio?)

Jim, NE0DA, has been taking advantage of the warmer weather and working on antennas. He is in the process of installing a G5RV dipole and painting a tripod to put on his roof. He discovered that the rotor for his HF beam has been damaged and will take some time to repair. He also was sporting a new Icom handheld at the meeting.

Stan, KC0OYO, also has installed a G5RV dipole and is putting his tower together at his new QTH.

Bill Dietrick, KD0ICM, reported that the wind broke his antenna again, so he has ordered a 2 meter Cushcraft Ringo vertical.

Bob, WD0FBW, is working on a problem in his vintage Heathkit HW-8.

Ed, NI5R, reported he recently worked a cruise ship off the western coast of Mexico, an aeronautical mobile at 35,000' off the coast of Georgia, and a man using an old military rig and running 15 watts from Tennessee. The club voted last month to buy the book to encourage Mr. Seip and his students to study for their licenses. Ed was very impressed with the youngsters who came to School Roundup.

Bill, W0NHF, has been exploring PSK-31 again.

Stanley, WB0JRG, recently purchased a Technician License Manual for two people who want to get their licenses.

Paul, KB0JIT, continues his work on vertical antennas and also helped at the School Roundup event at Skyview Middle School on Jan. 17. He called the kids "amazing."

New Business:

Tuesday Night Net

    Bill, WA0KAQ, stated that while the Tuesday night net usually has a good turnout, he is looking for ideas to make it more interesting and informative through some level of training. KB0JIT suggested that a short session of slow speed code practice might be useful, as some of the members have never used CW and have indicated an interest in it. WA0KAQ said he would take the idea into consideration.

    Bill also noted something that needs to be corrected by those checking in. "I would ask that you either stay until the end of the net once you check in, or that you tell the Net Control Station you are leaving the net after your comments are made. Several times we have had someone wanting to contact a station toward the end of the net, and the station does not come back to a call. If we had known the station was leaving, we could ask at the end of his comments whether anyone had anything for him before he checked out. This is just ordinary net courtesy, and we need to start doing this."

Net Control Stations

    "Are You Training Followers or Leaders?" was the title of an article in a recent issue of the ARES E-Letter. The article detailed how volunteers come out in force when a major event takes place, however the vast majority have to be trained before they can be of any use. Bill, WA0KAQ, said the same holds true in ham clubs. If the leadership suddenly disappears, who will have the knowledge and training to take over and do what is necessary?

    Bill acknowledged that more members than ever before are taking on responsibilities for the club this year, and that is as it should be. He praised those who participated in the School Roundup as a fine example of volunteers taking on a task and following through with it. Accomplishing tasks like this are great confidence builders.

    One of the club President's goals for 2011 is to train more members to serve as Net Control Station for our net. Bill noted that the net has grown to the point that it is not unusual to have 10-12 stations check in. Several members have supported the net by handling the Net Control Station duties, and the experience should be spread to more stations. He noted more members will be asked to occasionally call the net. "Once the first time jitters go away, it's not that difficult," he said. We all know one another on the net, so we are talking to friends. No one expects the net to run flawlessly each week, and if there is a little goof up, so what? It's a great way to learn to do things right the next time you call it.

    A few noted they couldn't hear all the stations. "That's what relays are for," Bill replied. "If you can't hear a station, someone else probably can and will tell you what he said. That's just part of running a net."

    Former club President Jim McConnell agreed. "It's not difficult," he said. "If you can read, you can call the net."
