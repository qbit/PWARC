NG0T reported a problem has arisen about the club's incorporation and its tax status with the IRS. Dan explained, "When we filed for a Tax ID Number, we indicated that we expected to be what is called a 501(c)(6), which is a non-profit, but not a 501(c)(3). A 501(c)(3) is a type of organization that you can make a donation to and write it off on your taxes. What I have since learned is that the only way to become either one of these is to file a form asking the IRS to bless your status - to officially say, 'We've looked at your paperwork and we're ok with you being a 501(c)(6) or a 501(c)(3).' A 501(c)(3) requires more paperwork than the other. The bad news in all of this is that to get them to sign off on your status requires an application fee with the application, and either one of these is the same fee, and it is pretty hefty -- $400 - which would wipe out our treasury. The good news is once you've done that you can electronically file an annual report as long as you don't take in more than $25,000 in a year. The report consists of little more than saying, 'We didn't take in more than $25,000, we're still here, here's our address and our contact point.'

"The problem we have right now is that our tax ID number is kind of in limbo. It's not officially associated with any kind of organization. I know that sounds odd, but what that means is that if I try to e-file something to that tax ID number that is non-profit, it will get kicked back. They are going to say, 'This isn't a non-profit of either type in our database' - even though we said when we filed for it 'This is what we expect we'll become.' So that's kind of the problem we face.

"The immediate problem is that if we're non-profit we need to file some type of form by Monday, May 17, for last year, or an extension. We really can't file either one because we aren't officially a non-profit. So, the best 'out' here that I can see is to file as a "c" corporation, which is kind of the default of what we would be as a corporation if we're not officially a non-profit. I can accomplish that. Then we, as a club, need to decide whether or not we're willing to go ahead with the $400 hit to the treasury, and which of these two routes we wish to go - whether we want to go to the extra trouble of becoming a 501(c)(3) or whether we want to go with 501(c)(6), which is intended for social clubs, basically."

Dan noted that with a 501(c)(6) corporation, we could take donations for anyone and they could take the donation off their taxes. If a business was donating, they couldn't take it off as a charitable donation, but they could take it off as a marketing or good will type expenditure.

"The other problem in all of this," Dan continued, "is that if we were a non-profit right now and we failed to file by Monday, there is a $20 a day penalty that kicks in if we don't file in time. I apologize for that, because I didn't become aware of that until a few days ago when I was looking at some of this material. I had thought that we had the luxury of no real penalties as long as there was no underlying tax. In fact, that is not the case.

"If we were to go in and file for either one of them now, and we came into existence officially on December 22, 2009, if they favorably said you're either one of these, then the problem is that you would have a late return that you are now filing where they could assert these penalties and you might have an uphill battle to get the penalty to go away.

"A 'c' corporation, which is the default position, actually was supposed to have filed back on March 15. However, if we take the approach that most of the money we took in before December 22 is initial capital, not income, then we would initially have a small loss for the year. The initial dues would be counted as capital contributions, not as income. That would be magical, because filing late on a 'c' corp where there is no net income, there is no tax and no penalty. I can file a 'c' corp return if that is agreeable to the club, to get us to a position that we have filed something and the IRS shouldn't be bothering us for non-filing grounds.

"I'm sure there are better things we could do with our limited resources than to pour it into that, although that would be a one-time-only expenditure.

"By going in as a 'c' corporation, we would essentially have most of the rest of the year to decide what we wanted to do. Even if we filed another 'c' corp again next year, it would probably only be a nominal tax on our revenue over our expenses. We could get rid of any tax by making sure we spend our treasury down at the end of the year on something we wanted to spend it on anyway.

"The question is whether everyone is agreeable to my filing the 'c' corp return so that we give IRS something and get them to not come asking why we haven't made so kind of a non-profit filing - which we effectively can't do until we get them to say we are some kind of non-profit."

KB0RPD made a motion that the club let Dan proceed with the filing for a 'c' corporation and we will look at the filing of a 501(c)(3) or 501(c)(6) later in the year. All members voted in the affirmative. The secretary will poll members by telephone until the number of affirmative votes constituting a quorum is obtained or until it is not possible to get the necessary number of positive votes.

KB0RPD reported the National Weather Service has more batteries ready for the club, but they have not yet been delivered. A few more will be needed before Field Day. Following that event, batteries will begin to be distributed to club members. The batteries work well for back-up power for transceivers, handhelds, small inverters and converters for computers, 12-volt lighting (especially LEDs), small AM/FM radios, 12-volt fans, etc.

KB0RPD requested the names and addresses of members who attended last month's meeting at which he presented the Weather Spotting program. Those who were in attendance are eligible to become Weather Spotters for the National Weather Service.

WA0KAQ reported he participated in a 2-hour Webinar in April pertaining to Field Day. One of the topics discussed was how to introduce ham radio to youth. A study showed that what interested youth most about amateur radio were satellites, DX, Morse code and Fox Hunting. One club went as far as to challenge a school class to choose someone for a texting versus CW contest, as made popular some time back on the Jay Leno Show. The prize was free pizza for the class if the student won. A local pizza shop donated the pizza for the publicity it received from the promotion. The class received the pizza in spite of its loss, but the stunt put ham radio operators in the classroom and gave them a chance to tell about their hobby as well.

NI5R and KB0RPD will work together to contact local schools to try to raise interest in our club working with the students. Perhaps something like the contest described could be done next fall.
