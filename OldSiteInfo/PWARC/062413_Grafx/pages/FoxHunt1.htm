Fox Hunting

An Inexpensive Tape Measure Yagi

Besides the requirement of having a radio that can receive signals from the Fox, the hunter will find a directional antenna to be an essential part of his equipment. The "Tape Measure Antenna" described below is both inexpensive and simple to build. It is made from a 1" metal tape measure and PVC pipe.

Parts List: (Drawings may be slightly different than the antenna being built, but the dimensions are the same. Drawing below shows 2 "X" connectors and a "T" connector. By replacing the "T" connector with another "X" connector, a handle can be put on front end to use when nulling a strong signal. The 1/2" pipe in the drawing has been replaced by 3/4" pipe for better stability. Hose clamps may be used on the reflector and director elements if desired.)

    36" - 3/4" Schedule 40 PVC pipe
    3 - 3/4" Schedule 40 PVC "X" fittings
    1 - 3/4" Schedule 40 PVC "EL" fitting (optional - to make vertical handle)
    2 to 6 - 1-1/2" Screw type muffler or hose clamps (2 required, 4 optional if director and reflector are screwed to the boom)
    1 - 16' long (or whatever size you can find) 1" wide metal tape measure or refill (Cheap is good!)
    1 - RG-58 or RG-8X coax cable at least 6' long
    1 - PL-259 coax connector
    1 - PL-259 to adapter for your radio (if needed)
    2 - Crimp on ring or spade connectors (size Blue recommended)
    6" - #20 or #18 copper wire for hair pin balun
    Electrical tape

SAFETY:

Care should be taken when working with sharp metal edges. Use gloves to prevent cuts and safety glasses to protect your eyes from flying debris. Care should also be used when cutting the metal tape as burrs created by the cuts could inflict injury.

STEP 1. Take the 36” PVC tube and cut one piece 11-1/2” long and another piece 7” long.

STEP 2. Assemble PVC tube pieces and PVC connectors (“T’s” and Crosses) as shown above. (No glue is used so antenna may be broken down for storage).

STEP 3. Remove the tape from the case. The easiest way to accomplish this is to pull the tape completely out until you see the metal return spring to which it is attached. Simply rotate the tape till the tab of the spring can be slid through the slot in the tape. The spring will recoil into the case and the tape will be ready for trimming.

STEP 4. Measure and cut the four lengths you will need for this antenna (cutting chart below). Use sharp sissors or side cutters to cut the tape.

    Part

    Length

    Reflective Element

    41-3/8"

    Driven Element (2 pieces)

    17-3/4"

STEP 5. File and sand any burrs or sharp edges left from cutting the tape.

STEP 6. Cover the exposed ends of the tape with plastic electrical tape. Remember: one end of each of the driven element pieces does not get covered.

STEP 7. On the untaped ends of the driven element, sand off the paint on the back side of the tape to expose about 1" of bare metal.

STEP 8. Center and attach the reflector and director elements to their appropriate locations on the PVC boom (see diagram) using the hose clamps (or alternate method demonstrated in the assembly notes).

STEP 9. Tin the ends of the driven element pieces with solder where you sanded the paint away. Then tin about ¼” of each end of the 6” wire you’ll be using for the “Hairpin” match. (You may have to strip the wire if it is jacketed). Also, tin the ends of the coax conductors at this time.

STEP 10. Place the driven element pieces on the center cross piece of the PVC boom, securing them with the hose clamps. You should leave about 1” spacing between the ends of the tape.

STEP 11. Place the coax center conductor and one end of the "Hairpin" match wife under the hose clamp to one driven element and the braid of the coax and the other end of the "Hairpin" match wire under the hose clamp of the other driven element.

STEP 12. Tighten both clamps on the driven elements.

STEP 13. Secure the coax to the boom with electrical tape. Your antenna will now function in the receive mode.

Now you’re ready to check your SWR with an SWR meter or an antenna analyzer if you plan to use it for transmitting. SWR adjustment is achieved by moving the driven elements closer or further away from each other.

 

ASSEMBLY NOTE: You may use a sheet metal screw and rubber washers to attach the reflector and director elements. This may save you a little bit of money.


     

The Finished Product

Jane, KA0USA, is proud of the performance from the tape measure antenna. She did most of the work herself, including cutting the PVC pipe with a chop saw and tinning the driven elements. The tape measure she used for the elements was purchased for $1.99 at Harbor Freight. She used 6 muffler clamps to attach the elements, rather than using screws to attach them to the PVC fittings. The ends of each element were covered with electrical tape. None of the PVC fittings are glued to the boom.

Jane's antenna shows good a front to back ratio and side signal rejection. Signal readings from the Colorado Connection's 145.130 repeater showed an S9+20 db (the highest the meter will show on our rig) with the antenna pointed at it, an S4 reading off the back of the antenna, and S0 (just noise) off the side of the antenna.

The original space between the driven elements was 1". We did tweak the SWR just a bit by decreasing the distance between the driven elements by 3/16", but as built, Jane's antenna was okay for use as a transmit antenna as well.

The length of the driven element could be shortened just a bit more, but it is quite usable as is. The SWR at 144 MHz was slightly over 1.3:1 at 144 MHz, less than 1.2:1 at 145.5 Megs, and less than 1.7:1 at 148 MHz. Any further tuning will be done by trimming the ends of each of the driven elements 1/16" at a time. No adjustment was made to the 6" hairpin match made from uninsulated #14 solid copper wire.

An 8' length of RG-58 was used to connect the antenna to the radio.

Tests have shown that the antenna does not function well in windy conditions, as the elements tend to flap in the breeze. However, in calm conditions the antenna works well and is reputed to have a gain of about 7 db. In combination with a suitable attenuator, it makes a formidable T-Hunt antenna.

Do not use the antenna to transmit unless you carefully adjust the “hairpin” and element length to give a low VSWR and an impedance very near 50 ohms.

For safety reasons, do not use more than 5-10 watts of power while hand holding this antenna. RF exposure to your body greater than recommended may result. For the same reason, do not point your antenna at anyone while you are transmitting!
